% ===================== WHY STUDY
\section*{Why Study Joint Random Variables?}

In science and in real life we are often interested in two (or more) random variables at the same time.

\subsection*{Examples}

\begin{itemize}
\item Height and weight of giraffes
\item IQ and birthweight of children
\item Frequency of exercise and rate of heart disease
\item Air pollution level and respiratory illness rate
\item Number of Facebook friends and age of members
\end{itemize}

Think: What kind of relationship would you expect in each case? Why?

\subsection*{Example \#1: Smart Transportation}

In a smart traffic system, two random variables:

\[
X = \text{Vehicle Speed}
\]

\[
Y = \text{Traffic Density}
\]

\textbf{Question:}

Can speed be high when the traffic density is also high?

Answer: Yes / No

So, $X$ and $Y$ are correlated random variables.

\textbf{Real ITS Application (probabilistic analysis)}

\begin{itemize}
\item Adaptive traffic signals
\item Congestion prediction
\item Autonomous vehicle routing
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Example \#2: Wireless Network Performance}

In a wireless system, two important random variables:

\[
X = \text{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)}
\]

\[
Y = \text{Data Throughput}
\]

\textbf{Why Joint Random Variables?}

Throughput depends on SNR (both vary due to fading, interference, and congestion).

\textbf{Question}

If SNR is high, will throughput always be high?

Answer: Yes / No

\textbf{Reasoning}

Network Congestion (conditional relationships)

\textbf{Network Applications}

\begin{itemize}
\item 5G scheduling
\item Adaptive modulation
\item Network planning
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Examples}

\textbf{Example \#3: Weather Prediction}

\[
X = \text{Rainfall}, \quad Y = \text{Temperature}
\]

Application: Weather forecasting

\textbf{Example \#4: Drone Delivery}

\[
X = \text{Wind Speed}, \quad Y = \text{Delivery Time}
\]

Application: Logistics and UAV systems

\textbf{Example \#5: Roll two dice}

Conditions:

This requires joint PMF.

Many examples exist involving multiple variables simultaneously.

\subsection*{Why Joint Distributions?}

In such situations the random variables have a joint distribution that allows us to:

\begin{itemize}
\item Compute probabilities of events involving both variables
\item Understand the relationship between the variables
\end{itemize}

Examples of events:

\[
X + Y = 7
\]

\[
X > Y
\]

This is simplest when the variables are independent.

When they are not independent, we use:

\begin{itemize}
\item Covariance
\item Correlation
\end{itemize}

to measure the nature of dependence between them.

% ===================== DISCRETE CASE
\section*{Discrete Case — Joint PMF}

\subsection*{Setup}

Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are discrete random variables.

\[
X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}
\]

\[
Y = \{y_1, y_2, \dots, y_m\}
\]

The ordered pair $(X,Y)$ takes values in the product set

\[
\{(x_1,y_1),(x_1,y_2),\dots,(x_n,y_m)\}
\]

\subsection*{Definition (Joint PMF)}

\[
p(x_i,y_j) = P(X = x_i, Y = y_j)
\]

This gives the probability of the joint outcome occurring simultaneously.

% ===================== TABLE
\subsection*{Joint PMF Table Representation}

We organize the joint PMF in a table.

\[
\begin{array}{c|cccc}
X \backslash Y & y_1 & y_2 & \dots & y_m \\ \hline
x_1 & p(x_1,y_1) & p(x_1,y_2) & \dots & p(x_1,y_m) \\
x_2 & p(x_2,y_1) & p(x_2,y_2) & \dots & p(x_2,y_m) \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
x_n & p(x_n,y_1) & p(x_n,y_2) & \dots & p(x_n,y_m)
\end{array}
\]

\subsection*{Key Properties}

\[
0 \le p(x_i,y_j) \le 1
\]

\[
\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{m} p(x_i,y_j) = 1
\]

% ===================== DICE EXAMPLE
\section*{Example: Rolling Two Dice}

\textbf{Experiment}

Roll two fair dice.

Let

\[
X = \text{value on the first die}
\]

\[
T = \text{total (sum) of both dice}
\]

Each outcome $(i,j)$ has probability

\[
\frac{1}{36}
\]

We construct the joint probability table of $(X,T)$.

\textbf{Observations}

Entries are either

\[
0 \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{1}{36}
\]

$X$ and $T$ are not independent.

% ===================== CONTINUOUS CASE
\section*{Continuous Case — Joint PDF}

Continuous case is analogous to the discrete case.

\begin{itemize}
\item Discrete values $\rightarrow$ Continuous intervals
\item Joint PMF $\rightarrow$ Joint PDF
\item Sums $\rightarrow$ Double integrals
\end{itemize}

If

\[
X \in [a,b], \quad Y \in [c,d]
\]

then the pair $(X,Y)$ takes values in

\[
[a,b] \times [c,d]
\]

\subsection*{Definition (Joint PDF)}

A function $f(x,y)$ is called the joint probability density function of $X$ and $Y$ if

\[
P((X,Y) \text{ lies in a small rectangle of area } dx\,dy)
\approx f(x,y)\,dx\,dy
\]

\subsection*{Key Properties}

\[
f(x,y) \ge 0
\]

\[
\int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = 1
\]

Note: The joint PDF may take values greater than 1.

It is a probability density, not a probability.

% ===================== EXAMPLE
\section*{Example}

Given

\[
f(x,y) = 4xy
\]

for

\[
0 \le x \le 1, \quad 0 \le y \le 1
\]

\textbf{Tasks}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Show that $f(x,y)$ is a valid joint PDF
\item Visualize the event
\item Compute $P(A)$
\end{enumerate}

where

\[
A = \{X < 0.5, Y > 0.5\}
\]

\subsection*{Step 1: Check Validity}

\[
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 4xy\,dx\,dy
\]

\[
= \int_0^1 \left(\int_0^1 4xy\,dx\right)dy
\]

\[
= \int_0^1 2y\,dy
\]

\[
= y^2 \Big|_0^1
\]

\[
= 1
\]

Thus $f(x,y) \ge 0$ and total probability is 1.

So it is a valid joint PDF.

\subsection*{Step 2: Compute $P(A)$}

\[
P(A) = \int_0^{0.5} \int_{0.5}^{1} 4xy\,dy\,dx
\]

\[
= \int_0^{0.5} [2xy^2]_{0.5}^{1} dx
\]

\[
= \int_0^{0.5} 2x(1 - 0.25)\,dx
\]

\[
= \int_0^{0.5} \frac{3}{2}x\,dx
\]

\[
= \frac{3}{16}
\]

% ===================== JOINT CDF
\section*{Joint Cumulative Distribution Function (Joint CDF)}

Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are jointly distributed random variables.

We use the notation

\[
X \le x, \quad Y \le y
\]

to mean the event

\[
\{X \le x \text{ and } Y \le y\}
\]

\subsection*{Definition}

\[
F(x,y) = P(X \le x, Y \le y)
\]

% ===================== CONTINUOUS CDF
\subsection*{Joint CDF — Continuous Case}

If $X$ and $Y$ are continuous random variables with joint density $f(x,y)$ over

\[
[a,b] \times [c,d]
\]

then

\[
F(x,y) = \int_a^x \int_c^y f(u,v)\,du\,dv
\]

\subsection*{Recovering the Joint PDF}

\[
f(x,y) =
\frac{\partial^2 F(x,y)}{\partial x \partial y}
\]

% ===================== DISCRETE CDF
\subsection*{Joint CDF — Discrete Case}

If $X$ and $Y$ are discrete random variables with joint PMF $p(x_i,y_j)$

\[
F(x,y) =
\sum_{x_i \le x}
\sum_{y_j \le y}
p(x_i,y_j)
\]

\textbf{Interpretation}

\begin{itemize}
\item Add all probabilities in the table
\item For rows with $x_i \le x$
\item And columns with $y_j \le y$
\end{itemize}

% ===================== PROPERTIES
\section*{Properties of the Joint CDF}

\subsection*{1. Non-decreasing}

If $x$ or $y$ increase, then $F(x,y)$ must stay constant or increase.

\subsection*{2. Lower-left Corner}

\[
F(x,y) = 0
\]

at the lower-left of the joint range.

If the lower-left corner is

\[
(-\infty,-\infty)
\]

then

\[
\lim_{(x,y)\to(-\infty,-\infty)} F(x,y) = 0
\]

\subsection*{3. Upper-right Corner}

\[
F(x,y) = 1
\]

at the upper-right of the joint range.

If the upper-right corner is

\[
(\infty,\infty)
\]

then

\[
\lim_{(x,y)\to(\infty,\infty)} F(x,y) = 1
\]
